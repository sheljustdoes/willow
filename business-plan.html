Phase 2
02

Professional Liability Insurance

Non-negotiable. Purchase this before the first client conversation. Families are relying on your guidance — you need to be protected.

Week 2–3

Any smart business person knows to protect themselves. All practicing advocates need liability insurance — professional liability, E&O, and a business owners policy.

— Alliance of Professional Health Advocates
🛡️

Primary carrier: CPH & Associates

The most commonly recommended carrier in the patient advocacy community. Policies are issued within minutes online. Covers claims, regulatory board grievances, unlimited legal defense, personal injury, Good Samaritan liability. Occurrence-based (not claims-made) — meaning you're covered for incidents even after the policy expires. Get a quote at cphins.com/patient-advocate

⚖️

Professional Liability / E&O

Covers claims arising from your advisory services. $1M per claim / $3M aggregate is standard. This is your core policy.

🏢

General Liability

Covers bodily injury or property damage — for example, a client trips during an in-person meeting. Add as a rider to CPH.

🔐

Cyber Liability

Critical because you handle PHI (protected health information). Covers data breaches and HIPAA-related privacy incidents. Add to your CPH policy.

Get a quote from CPH & Associates at cphins.com/patient-advocate
Online application takes ~10 minutes. Policy issued within minutes of approval. Target $1M/$3M limits.

BCPA Certification

Board Certified Patient Advocate — the gold-standard credential for independent advocacy. Validates the expertise clients are already trusting you with.

Months 2–4
Visit pacboard.org and read the full Candidate Handbook
The handbook covers all eligibility requirements, the 5 exam domains, timeline, and policies. Reading it is required as part of the application.
Start hereFree
Determine your eligibility pathway
Option A: Bachelor's degree or higher. Option B: Equivalent experience (paid or volunteer) documented with a 250–500 word narrative citing PACB's Knowledge, Skills & Abilities. Your 20+ years of nursing qualifies strongly for the experience pathway.
Confirm pathway
Secure two letters of recommendation
Must come from people who have directly observed your advocacy work — former colleagues, physicians you've worked alongside, or supervisors. Character references that validate your patient advocacy skills.
Reach out early
Consider applying for the PACB scholarship
Covers the $425 exam fee entirely for first-time candidates. Application deadlines: May 31 for Fall cycle, Oct 31 for Spring cycle. pacboard.org/bcpa-scholarship

Financial Infrastructure

Getting paid cleanly, staying compliant with the IRS, and never getting surprised by a tax bill. Build this early.

Month 1 onward
Set up bookkeeping software immediately
Wave Accounting is free and works well for a solo practice. QuickBooks Self-Employed is $15/mo and integrates with tax software. Start tracking from day one — do not try to reconstruct expenses later.
Start nowFree–$15/mo
Free
Find a CPA familiar with solo healthcare practices
Ask your APHA network for referrals. The first year consultation is the most important — they'll help you set up correctly, determine your quarterly payment amounts, and identify healthcare-specific deductions.
Year 1 setup$500–$1,500/yr
~$800/yr
Set up quarterly estimated tax payments
As an LLC owner, you pay self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings) plus income tax. Quarterly deadlines: April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15. Set aside ~30% of every payment received into a separate savings account.
Avoid surprisesQuarterly
Set up a payment system for client invoicing
Square, Stripe, or Wave Payments all work. Include an invoice template with your LLC name, EIN, and clear payment terms (Net 7 or Net 14). Decide your pricing model: hourly ($100–$175/hr) vs. package-based.
Before first client
Document your startup expenses as tax deductions
LLC filing fees, insurance, certification costs, website, materials, home office, mileage — all deductible as business expenses. Keep every receipt. IRS allows deducting up to $5,000 in startup costs in Year 1.
